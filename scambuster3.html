Scam Buster

The lure of instant cash has tempted many "ner do wells" to attempt to rip-off, scam and defraud Fixed Odds Betting Terminals up and down the country. We will now list a few of the failed attempts that have been sent in by Bookmakers and staff, who we thank for this information.

Strimming

For Years the slot machine industry has been victim to "strimmers", this involves people attaching a pound coin to a piece of wire, commonly used is the type of wire found on garden strimmers. As technology progresses the bad boys progress with it, inventing more advanced ways of obtaining fraudulent credits on slot machines, and with the advent of fixed odds betting terminals utilizing not only note acceptors but coin validators this has left some machines sitting ducks to teams of strimmers, especially bearing in mind the jackpot on a standard slot machine is £25 with an average float of around £200, the FOBT holds no float but has the ability to payout via the ticket receipt unlimited amounts of money, linked to the fact that you can cash-out any money inserted into the machines.

News has been passed to us that there is a problem at the moment with a certain type of FOBT coin mechanism (which we wont mention for security reasons) and one or more teams of strimmers are exploiting this vulnerability.

Any operator of either Slot machines or FOBT's who is experiencing cash losses from strimming is advised to contact Gary Pettengell from Machineguard UK Ltd.

Gary Pettengell a Great Yarmouth policeman whose expertise is solving new trends in crime has been granted special dispensation by the Norfolk Police Authority to wage a campaign against machine fraud. The battle is expected to expand on a nationwide scale.

Gary Pettengell began his original scheme in Great Yarmouth about four years ago. The outcome has been a 50 per cent reduction in amusement arcade crime in the resort, but elsewhere the story is less encouraging as theft faces an increase.

“Robberies started to escalate on a national basis with organised gangs well equipped with special tools and a wide knowledge of the workings of fruit machines. If the thieves were seen on CCTV cameras, it became unlikely they would be recognised because they were not local people.”

A national scheme was needed and Pettengell found that more of his time was dedicated to beating what was a growing crime wave. Eventually, with the approval of his police employers, he founded Machineguard UK Ltd, with himself as managing director.
